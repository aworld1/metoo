Women are more likely to live in povertyclose

Women are 35% more likely than men to live in poverty.
- 1 in 8 women lived in poverty in 2015.
- 1 in 3 single mothers lived in poverty in 2015.
- Of the nation’s 14.5 million poor children, more than half live in families headed by women.
Women are not paid equally relative to men, and therefore experience this hardship.
According to the source, "reducing poverty for women will require us to invest in support systems, employment initiatives, and most importantly, education" (Your Dream).

Women are far more likely to be victims of domestic violenceclose

Shocking statistics directs society's attention to domestic violence. These statistics, according to the source, are:
- 4 in 5 victims of domestic violence are women.
- Each year, 4.7 million American women experience physical violence by an intimate partner.
- 3 women on average are murdered by a boyfriend or husband each day.
- Domestic violence costs the economy between $10-67 billion each year in health care services and lost worker productivity.

This new research that shows gender roles in domestic abuse pressures society into staying away from such violence in future events and encounters.

Education meets equalityclose

The source states the following:

- 84% of women age 25 and over with a high school diploma or more equals the percentage for men.
- 30% of young women, ages 25 to 29, had completed college as of 2000, which exceeded the 28% of their male counterparts who had done so. Young women also had higher high school completion rates than young men: 89% versus 87%.

The college degree attainment gap between the sexes had not closed completely, but it was closing. In 2000, 24% of women age 25 and over had a bachelor's degree or higher, compared with 28% of men.

Women break the computer science stereotypeclose

Since the birth of the computer, women have been stereotyped to being disassociated with technology and advancement. But in turn, the new generation of women have risen up to trump the sstereotype.

70% The percentage of women with access to a computer at home in 1997 who used it; the rate for men was 72%.

Equal Pay Actmore

Source

Equal Pay Actclose

According to the source, during 1963 the act "passed by Congress, promising equitable wages for the same work, regardless of the race, color, religion, national origin or sex of the worker." This act was one of the many acts released in the 60's that supported women's rights after decades of ignorance to the act's human rights. Although this act is in place, there is still a 20%-30% margin of pay between genders, the female side statistically inferior.

Civil Rights Actmore

Source

Civil Rights Actclose

Building on top of the Equal Pay Act one year later, the Civil Rights Act prohibits "sex discrimination in employment." This act layers on top of the previous because of the acts' similar goals to eliminate sex discrimination in the workplace.

Acts are reinforcedmore

Source

Acts are reinforcedclose

Four years after the Civil Rights Act, which occurred in 1964, "President Lyndon B. Johnson signs an executive order prohibiting sex discrimination by government contractors and requiring affirmative action plans for hiring women." This was the final layer of the acts, for it enforced the acts by the executive branch while being supported by the legislative branch.
